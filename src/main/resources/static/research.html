Understanding Stock Analysis

Opening Price

The opening price is the first trade of the day at market open (9:30 AM ET). It often differs from the previous closing price due to after-hours news, earnings, or events that happened while markets were closed. A large gap between close and open is called a gap up (positive) or gap down (negative) and can signal strong momentum.

Closing Price

The closing price (4:00 PM ET) is considered the most important price of the day. Portfolio values, performance metrics, and technical indicators are all calculated using closing prices. Institutional investors — mutual funds, hedge funds, pension funds — often make decisions based on where a stock closes relative to key levels.

Volume

Volume is the total number of shares traded during the session. It answers the question: how much conviction is behind this price move?

  • A price increase on high volume suggests strong buying interest — the move is more likely to continue.
  • A price increase on low volume may not be sustainable — fewer participants are driving the move.
  • Unusually high volume (2–3× the daily average) often precedes or accompanies major news events.
  • Institutional investors (funds managing billions) must trade large volumes gradually — their activity shows up as sustained volume over days.

1-Day Change

How much the stock has moved since yesterday's close, shown as both a dollar amount and a percentage. The percentage is more meaningful — a $5 move means very different things for a $10 stock vs. a $500 stock.

  • Under ±1%: Typical day, low volatility
  • ±1–3%: Notable move, likely some news or sector activity
  • ±3–5%: Significant — earnings, guidance, analyst upgrades/downgrades
  • Over ±5%: Major event — earnings beat/miss, FDA approval, acquisition news

For reference: the S&P 500 index averages roughly +0.04% per day over the long run.

Performance Periods (1W, 1M, 3M, 6M, YTD)

Each period shows how much the stock has gained or lost from that point in time until today. These help you understand the stock's momentum across different time horizons:

  • 1 Week: Short-term momentum. Useful for spotting reactions to recent news.
  • 1 Month: Aligns with monthly reporting cycles. A widely-used benchmark period.
  • 3 Months: One earnings cycle. The market's verdict on recent quarterly results often shows here.
  • 6 Months: Filters short-term noise. Sustained trends are more visible at this scale.
  • YTD (Year-to-Date): From January 1st to today. Used to compare against indices like the S&P 500 (+/- the market's annual performance).

A stock outperforming the S&P 500 in every period shows strong relative strength — a signal many professional investors watch closely.

What a Price Chart Shows

A price chart plots a stock's closing price over time. The x-axis is time; the y-axis is price. The shape of the line tells a story about investor sentiment and supply/demand dynamics.

Trends

  • Uptrend: A series of higher highs and higher lows. Buyers are in control. Each dip gets bought up before reaching the previous low.
  • Downtrend: Lower highs and lower lows. Sellers are in control. Each rally fails before reaching the previous high.
  • Sideways (consolidation): The stock moves within a range. Neither buyers nor sellers have conviction. Often precedes a breakout in one direction.

Support and Resistance

Support is a price level where the stock tends to stop falling — buyers step in at that price. Resistance is where it tends to stop rising — sellers take profits there.

When a stock breaks through resistance on high volume, that resistance often becomes the new support — a bullish sign. The reverse is true for breaking support.

What to Look For

  • Sharp single-day spikes: Usually news-driven. Sustainable or not depends on volume and what follows.
  • Smooth, gradual climbs: Often driven by improving fundamentals. More sustainable than vertical moves.
  • V-shaped recovery: A sharp drop followed by an equally sharp recovery. Can indicate an overreaction to temporary news.
  • Staircase pattern: Move up → consolidate → move up → consolidate. Classic institutional accumulation pattern.

Time Frame Matters

The same stock can look like a great buy on a 1-week chart and a poor one on a 1-year chart — or vice versa. Always look at multiple time frames. Short-term traders focus on days/weeks; long-term investors focus on months/years. Understanding your own time horizon is critical.

Bull Market

A period of rising stock prices, typically defined as a 20%+ gain from a recent low. Characterized by investor optimism and economic expansion. The average bull market lasts about 4 years.

Bear Market

A period of falling prices — 20%+ decline from a recent high. Often tied to economic recession or contraction. The average bear market lasts about 14 months. "Bear" because a bear swipes down.

Market Capitalization

Share price × total shares outstanding. Represents the total market value of a company. Large-cap (>$10B) are stable blue chips; mid-cap ($2–10B) offer growth potential; small-cap (<$2B) are riskier but can grow faster.

P/E Ratio

Price-to-Earnings ratio: share price ÷ earnings per share. A P/E of 20 means investors pay $20 for every $1 of annual earnings. High P/E = growth expectations; low P/E = value or concern. Compare within the same industry.

Dividend

A portion of a company's profits paid directly to shareholders, usually quarterly. Mature, profitable companies (utilities, consumer staples) tend to pay dividends. Growth companies (tech startups) typically reinvest profits instead.

Volatility

How much a stock's price fluctuates. High volatility = big swings up and down (higher risk, higher potential reward). Low volatility = steady, predictable movement. Measured statistically as standard deviation or "beta."

Beta

Measures a stock's volatility relative to the market. Beta of 1.0 = moves with the market. Beta of 1.5 = 50% more volatile than the market. Beta of 0.5 = half as volatile. Negative beta = moves opposite to the market (rare).

52-Week High / Low

The highest and lowest prices over the past year. Trading near a 52-week high can mean momentum; near a 52-week low may signal trouble or a value opportunity. Context (why it reached those levels) matters more than the number itself.

Earnings Per Share (EPS)

Net profit ÷ total shares outstanding. Shows how much profit the company generates per share. Rising EPS over time is a sign of a healthy, growing business and is one of the most important fundamental metrics.

ETF (Exchange-Traded Fund)

A basket of securities traded on an exchange like a single stock. An S&P 500 ETF (e.g., SPY) gives you exposure to 500 companies in one purchase. ETFs offer instant diversification and typically lower fees than actively managed funds.

Diversification

Spreading investments across different assets, sectors, and geographies to reduce risk. If one sector crashes, others may hold steady. "Don't put all your eggs in one basket." Research shows diversification reduces risk without proportionally reducing returns.

Liquidity

How easily a stock can be bought or sold without significantly affecting its price. High-volume, large-cap stocks are highly liquid. Thinly-traded small-cap stocks can have wide spreads and large price impact when you try to buy or sell.

Short Selling

Borrowing shares and selling them, hoping the price drops so you can buy them back cheaper and pocket the difference. Unlimited downside risk (price can rise indefinitely). Used by hedge funds and sophisticated investors to profit from or hedge against declines.

Earnings Report

Published quarterly by public companies. Shows revenue, expenses, profit, and forward guidance. Earnings reports are the most significant recurring event affecting stock prices. "Beating estimates" (earning more than analysts predicted) typically causes a stock to rise.

Market Order vs Limit Order

A market order executes immediately at whatever the current price is. A limit order only executes at a price you specify or better — giving you control over the price but no guarantee of execution.

Dollar-Cost Averaging (DCA)

Investing a fixed dollar amount on a regular schedule regardless of price. When prices are low you buy more shares; when high, fewer. Over time this reduces the impact of volatility and removes the impossible task of "timing the market."
